Frequently Asked Questions

What is the US Navy body fat method?

The US Navy method estimates body fat using circumference measurements — neck, waist, and (for women) hips — along with height. It uses a logarithmic formula developed by the Department of Defense and is more accurate than BMI-based estimates because it accounts for body shape rather than just weight. The Navy uses it to assess fitness standards for service members.

How accurate is the Navy method vs DEXA or calipers?

The Navy method typically has a margin of error of ±3–4% compared to DEXA (dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry), which is considered the gold standard. Skinfold calipers done by a trained professional are similarly accurate at ±3–4%. The BMI method is less accurate because it doesn't distinguish between fat and muscle mass. For most people, the Navy method is a solid, free alternative to lab-based methods.

Where exactly do I measure for the Navy method?

Measure at the narrowest point of your neck (just below the larynx, sloping downward). Measure your waist at the naval (belly button) level for men, or at the narrowest point between the ribs and hips for women. Women also measure hips at the widest point. Take all measurements relaxed (not flexed), and measure twice to confirm accuracy.

What is a healthy body fat percentage?

For men: Essential fat 2–5%, Athletes 6–13%, Fitness 14–17%, Acceptable 18–24%, Obese 25%+. For women: Essential fat 10–13%, Athletes 14–20%, Fitness 21–24%, Acceptable 25–31%, Obese 32%+. Women naturally carry more essential fat due to hormonal and reproductive needs. These ranges are general guidelines — your ideal range may vary by age and individual health factors.

What is lean body mass?

Lean body mass (LBM) is everything in your body that isn't fat — muscles, bones, organs, water, and connective tissue. It's calculated as: LBM = Total Weight × (1 − Body Fat %). Tracking lean mass is often more useful than tracking total weight when you're exercising, because you may gain muscle while losing fat, keeping total weight similar.

How is the BMI method different?

The BMI method estimates body fat from your Body Mass Index (weight ÷ height²), age, and sex using a regression formula. It's less accurate than the Navy method because BMI doesn't differentiate muscle from fat — a muscular athlete may show "obese" BMI. Use it as a rough estimate only; the Navy method is recommended for better accuracy.
